Section 01 — Intial Emissions Data
The TAS Random Forest Model

The first step of our project was to generate surface air temperature projection data for each considered year (2015-2100) along each pathway. In quarter one of the capstone, we already generated this data for each pathway using a random forest regression model taking in global mean emissions of Carbon Dioxide, Sulfur Dioxide, Methane, and Black Carbon. We then converted this data, which was stored in .nc files, into .csv files for future ease of access and use. For each pathway, there's one file, containing the global mean of the surface air temperature and the associated year, spanning from 2015, our base year, to our final goal year of 2100.

Section 02 — The Brick Model
Projecting Sea Level Rise

To project sea level rise, we used the BRICK (Building blocks for Relevant Ice and Climate Knowledge) model, chosen for its modularity and compatibility with our input data. BRICK decomposes global sea level rise into four physical components: thermosteric (thermal) expansion, glaciers and small ice caps, the Greenland Ice Sheet, and the Antarctic Ice Sheet, each modeled separately based on surface air temperature projections.


Since sea level rise is not spatially uniform, we applied sea level fingerprints to translate global component contributions into regional projections. These fingerprints capture the gravitational, rotational, and deformational effects of ice mass redistribution; for example, regions near melting ice sheets can experience local sea level fall, while far-field regions see amplified rise. Thermosteric expansion is treated as spatially uniform.


We ran this pipeline independently for each of our four SSP scenarios (SSP1-2.6, SSP2-4.5, SSP3-7.0, SSP5-8.5), feeding temperature time series into each BRICK component and applying the appropriate fingerprints to produce annual regional sea level projections from 2015 to 2100. These gridded outputs then serve as the primary physical input for our economic damage estimation. Section 03 — The PyCIAM Model
Projecting the Economic Costs of Sea Level Rise

To estimate economic damages, we used the Data-Spatial Climate Impact Model (DSCIM) platform, an open-source framework that combines spatial and economic data to quantify the consequences of sea level rise. It consists of two main inputs and one core model: the SLIIDERS dataset, our BRICK sea level projections, and the pyCIAM model.


SLIIDERS (Sea Level Impacts Input Dataset by Elevation, Region, and Scenario) provides the foundational data for each coastal segment globally. It includes physical attributes such as inundation areas, extreme sea level heights, and wetland coverage, as well as socioeconomic variables like population, GDP, and capital stock. We supplemented this with our BRICK sea level projections by matching SSP scenarios and geographic coordinates, imputing missing values from the nearest available location.


The pyCIAM model processes these inputs to determine the most economically efficient response for each coastal segment under rising seas. It outputs six cost categories: permanent inundation, storm damage to capital, mortality, coastal protection expenditures, relocation costs, and wetland loss. The model evaluates three adaptation strategies: reactive retreat (relocating people and capital after land falls below sea level), coastal protection (building infrastructure scaled to projected sea level rise), and proactive retreat (preemptive relocation based on future sea level projections). Costs are computed across multi-decade planning periods, with adaptation decisions updated based on the maximum projected sea level in each period.
