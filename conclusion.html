Quantifying the Economic Impacts of Sea Level Rise

Conclusion

Our results highlight the long-term economic significance of sea level rise and reinforce the importance of both climate mitigation and adaptation in reducing future damages.

Section 01 — Implications
Why these results matter

This project shows that sea level rise is not only an environmental problem, but also a substantial economic issue. Across all scenarios, rising sea levels create measurable costs through flooding, land loss, infrastructure damage, and adaptation expenses. These damages increase substantially under higher-emissions pathways, showing that future economic outcomes depend strongly on today’s climate decisions.


Our results also suggest that the impacts of sea level rise are uneven across space. Some coastal regions face far greater damages than others, meaning that climate risk is not distributed equally. This makes it important to use regional projections and locally informed adaptation strategies rather than relying only on global averages.


More broadly, this work demonstrates the value of connecting physical climate models to socioeconomic models. By linking projected sea level rise to economic damages, we can better understand the stakes of climate change and provide more concrete information for planning and policy.

Even under lower-emissions pathways, some sea level rise remains unavoidable, making adaptation planning essential alongside emissions reductions.

Section 02 — What Can We Do About Sea Level Rise?
Mitigation and adaptation strategies

Responding to sea level rise requires a combination of mitigation and adaptation. Mitigation focuses on reducing greenhouse gas emissions in order to limit the severity of long-term warming and future sea level rise. The lower damages associated with more moderate SSP scenarios in our results reinforce the value of policies that reduce emissions over time.


Adaptation is equally important because some amount of sea level rise is already locked in. Coastal protection infrastructure, restoration of natural barriers such as wetlands, improved zoning and land-use planning, and in some cases managed retreat can help reduce future damages. These strategies are often costly, but our findings suggest that delaying action may lead to even larger long-term costs.


In practice, addressing sea level rise means investing early, planning locally, and making decisions that account for uncertainty. By combining scientific projections with economic modeling, this project provides one way to support those decisions with evidence.

Section 03 — Stakeholders
Who can use these results?

A wide range of stakeholders can benefit from this analysis. Policymakers and government agencies can use economic damage estimates to inform coastal planning, infrastructure investment, and climate adaptation policy. Urban planners and engineers can use regional projections to identify vulnerable coastal areas and prioritize resilience measures.


Communities living in coastal zones are also major stakeholders, since they are among the groups most directly affected by flooding, land loss, and relocation pressures. Businesses, insurers, and property owners may also use this information to better understand long-term exposure to climate risk.


Finally, researchers, advocacy organizations, and educators can use these findings to communicate the urgency of sea level rise and support evidence-based action. The broader goal of this project is not only to estimate damages, but also to contribute to the growing understanding of why early climate action matters.
